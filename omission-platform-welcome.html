What This Is & How To Use It

Most governance tools measure
what policies say.
OMISSION measures what
decision-makers actually see.

A risk that exists but is not seen is an omission. This platform quantifies the structural gap between documented coverage and actual visibility — and turns that gap into an auditable score.

The Problem It Solves

Standard governance audits ask: "Do you have a policy for X?" That question has a fatal flaw. A policy that exists but is invisible to the people responsible for acting on it provides no real protection. The harm still happens. The liability still accumulates. The audit still passes.

OMISSION asks the harder question: even if the policy exists — how well can the people who need it actually see it? It measures this gap across eight governance domains and surfaces the difference as a quantified risk score, not a checkbox.

The measurement model Risk Delta = True Score − Current Score
True Score   = Σ H × (1 − W)   // harm-weighted blind spots
Current Score = Σ H × W           // what decision-makers perceive

H is harm potential (0–1): how badly this domain damages the organisation if it fails. W is visibility weight (0.01–1): how clearly decision-makers actually see its risks. The gap between what H says is at stake and what W says is being seen is your omission risk.

The Eight Domains

Every assessment scores across the same eight governance areas:

Identity Process Data Risk Compliance Ethics Oversight Continuity

Each domain gets its own H and W rating. A domain with high harm potential and low visibility is your most urgent blind spot — regardless of whether your policy documentation for it is complete.

What You Get Out

Output Current Score Governance health as perceived by decision-makers (0–100)
Output True Score Governance health based on actual harm potential (0–100)
Output Risk Delta The gap between what you perceive and what is actually at risk
Output Omission Risk Index 0–100 index of risk hiding in blind spots across all domains

How To Use It

There are two modes. Start with Quick Assessment. Use Deep Scan when you have documents.

  1. Open the tool No account, no installation, no data sent anywhere. Everything runs in your browser. Open the live demo and you are already inside the platform.
  2. Choose Quick Assessment (10 minutes) Rate each of the eight domains yourself. Set H — how badly this domain could harm the organisation if it fails (0–1). Set W — how clearly decision-makers currently see that risk (0.01–1). The scores calculate in real time.
  3. Or run Deep Scan with your documents Upload governance documents — policies, frameworks, board reports. An AI model reads them and returns evidence-based H and W scores per domain, grounded in what the documents actually say rather than what you assume they say.
  4. Read the Risk Delta, not just the score A high Current Score with a high Risk Delta is the most dangerous result. It means decision-makers believe they are well-governed while the actual exposure is large. That gap is the omission.
  5. Prioritise by harm-weighted visibility Domains with high H and low W are your action list. Not the domains with the most documentation — the domains where the stakes are highest and the sight lines are shortest.
Deep Scan requires an API key. You can use Claude (Anthropic), GPT-4o (OpenAI), or Gemini (Google). Keys are held in browser memory only and never sent to this server. Get a key at console.anthropic.com, platform.openai.com, or aistudio.google.com. If running locally, Claude requires a local server due to CORS — run python3 -m http.server 8080 and open localhost:8080/omission-platform.html.

Who It Is For

OMISSION is designed for anyone who needs to answer the question a standard audit cannot: not whether governance exists, but whether it is seen. That includes governance leads running self-assessments, board members stress-testing what leadership actually has visibility over, researchers studying the structural gap between policy documentation and decision-maker awareness, and anyone preparing evidence for an accountability review.

It is not a compliance tool. It does not tell you whether your policies meet a regulatory standard. It tells you whether the people responsible for acting on those policies can see them clearly enough to act. That is a different and more difficult question.
